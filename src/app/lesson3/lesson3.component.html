Chapter 3: The Many Uses of Credit

Productive Business Loans

At the heart of economic growth are business loans that expand production. Able’s loan to Baker and Charlie to help them build fishing nets is an example of a productive business loan. These types of loans typically help individuals and businesses increase their capacity to produce goods and services, which benefits society by enhancing overall productivity.

However, as mentioned in the text, there’s a risk involved. Not every business venture will succeed, and in the case where Baker and Charlie fail to produce effective fishing nets, the loan may be wasted, which leads to a loss of saved resources.

Real-life Example:

Startups and Venture Capital: When investors like venture capitalists (VCs) provide funding to a startup, they are taking a risk. The startup may succeed and create a product or service that benefits society, or it may fail, resulting in the loss of the investment.

Consumption Loans

A consumption loan is different from a business loan in that it’s used for immediate consumption rather than for expanding productive capacity. For example, if Able had lent Baker and Charlie fish to take a vacation, it would be a consumption loan.

While Able may receive the interest on such a loan, there’s no increase in the productive capacity of the island. After the vacation, Baker and Charlie would still only be able to catch one fish per day.

Real-life Example:

Personal Loans for Consumption: A consumer loan is often used to purchase goods or services that do not directly increase a person’s ability to generate income. For example, taking out a loan to buy a car for personal use may make life more convenient but doesn’t lead to increased income generation.

The Dangers of Consumption Loans

The chapter also warns against loans that don’t expand productive capacity, like the vacation loan Baker and Charlie request. While such loans may seem harmless, they can result in hardship if the borrower is unable to repay the loan.

Real-life Example:

Government Bailouts and Subsidies: Government bailouts and subsidies are sometimes given to businesses or sectors that are struggling, but these often fail to lead to long-term improvements in the economy.

Emergency Loans

An emergency loan is a type of loan given in a crisis to maintain basic survival, such as when Baker and Charlie fall ill due to the Pokalani Pox. Emergency loans serve a crucial role in preventing the collapse of society’s productive capacity.

Real-life Example:

Disaster Relief and Emergency Loans: After natural disasters, governments and financial institutions often offer emergency loans to affected individuals or businesses. For instance, after Hurricane Katrina in 2005, the U.S. government provided emergency loans to businesses and homeowners in New Orleans to help them rebuild.

The Reality Check: Can Credit Be Expanded?

A critical point raised in the chapter is whether credit can be expanded by simply increasing the amount of money available. In the case of Able’s fishing business, he can only lend as much fish as he has saved.

Real-life Example:

Quantitative Easing: In the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis, central banks around the world engaged in quantitative easing (QE). While QE increased the money supply, it didn’t automatically result in more productive investment.

The Role of Government in Allocating Credit

Governments often intervene in the credit market by offering guarantees, subsidies, and incentives for certain types of loans. However, such interventions can distort the allocation of resources.

Real-life Example:

Student Loans and Housing Market: The U.S. government has provided subsidies and guarantees for student loans and home mortgages. While these policies have increased access to education and home ownership, they have also contributed to student debt crises and housing bubbles.

Takeaways

  • Productive loans expand the economy.
  • Consumption loans don’t add value to society.
  • Emergency loans are necessary in crises.
  • Government interventions often distort credit allocation.
  • Credit is limited by real savings, not just the money supply.
