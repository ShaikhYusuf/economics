CHAPTER 7: Infrastructure and Trade

The Importance of Infrastructure

Traditionally, islanders collected drinking water from mountain streams and carried it to their huts in vessels they fashioned. This limited productivity and created challenges during droughts.

To address this, Able Fisher V devised a system of reservoirs, funded by Maxine Goodbank, enabling efficient water storage and usage. This infrastructure transformed the island's economy by improving farming and industry.

Real-Life Example: The Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)
The TVA in the U.S., created in the 1930s, addressed flooding and lack of electricity, boosting agriculture, industry, and living standards in the Tennessee River Valley.

Trade and Economic Growth

Trade flourished as the island exported goods and imported necessities. This mirrored comparative advantage, where islands specialized in what they did best.

Real-Life Example: Global Trade in Electronics and Natural Resources
Nations like China and South Korea export electronics while importing raw materials, benefiting from comparative advantage and fueling economic growth.

Real-Life Example: Free Trade Agreements (FTA)
Agreements like NAFTA eliminated tariffs, fostering efficient trade and economic growth by focusing on each country's strengths.

Reality Check: Big vs. Little Economics

Infrastructure spending can boost economies but must balance costs and benefits. Poorly planned projects waste resources and hinder growth.

Real-Life Example: The "Bridge to Nowhere"
A costly and unnecessary bridge in Alaska became a symbol of wasteful government spending, highlighting the importance of productive infrastructure investments.

Protectionism vs. Free Trade

Trade barriers harm consumers by limiting access to affordable goods and reducing efficiency. Nations benefit by focusing on their competitive advantages.

Real-Life Example: Tariffs on Steel
U.S. steel tariffs raised costs for industries reliant on steel, hurting competitiveness and consumers while protecting inefficient industries.

Real-Life Example: The U.S. Trade Deficit
The U.S. struggles with a trade deficit due to challenges in manufacturing. Investing in competitive sectors can help address this imbalance.

Takeaway

Infrastructure spending and trade policies are key to economic growth, but their success depends on careful planning and leveraging comparative advantages.
